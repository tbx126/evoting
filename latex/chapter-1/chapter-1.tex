Electronic voting systems promise rapid vote tabulation, expanded voter accessibility, and auditable records capable of withstanding independent scrutiny. These advantages have kept the technology prominent in electoral reform discussions for decades.

Yet turning those promises into practice has proven far more difficult than expected.

A functional electronic voting system must uphold ballot secrecy, correctly enforce state transitions, provide sufficient transparency for independent verification, and remain practical for election officials and voters alike. These competing demands create tensions that explain why so many implementations stumble.

Centralized platforms deliver smooth user experiences and fast processing. Even so, they place the entire burden of trust on backend operators. Those operators alone control the audit logs, manage state changes, and release the final tallies. Independent checks reach only as far as the operators choose to allow. In disputed elections, investigators quickly hit a wall of restricted access, with no publicly verifiable path forward.

This dissertation pursues a different approach. It shifts the basis of assurance away from institutional trust and onto on-chain enforcement supported by cryptographic proofs, while keeping key custody as the sole explicit trust assumption.

The architecture combines ElGamal homomorphic encryption for privacy-preserving tally computation, Groth16 zero-knowledge proofs for efficient verification, and commitment-based Merkle inclusion proofs to enable post-election inclusion verification, all linked in a single end-to-end pipeline.

The work goes beyond a purely theoretical outline. The resulting prototype compiles successfully, supports deployment on blockchain test networks, has undergone targeted testing, and is available for independent review.

\section{Problem Statement}

Constructing a voting system that minimises dependence on trusted operators requires satisfying a collection of requirements that do not fully align.

Ballot confidentiality must hold across submission, storage, and counting. Correctness checks must validate vote formation and tally claims without disclosing ballot contents. Election state transitions require tamper-evident enforcement. Voters, in turn, need a verification path that confirms inclusion using a local receipt together with a published audit bundle. That bundle must also support a root-consistency check against the on-chain \texttt{merkleRoot}, without any address-based lookup during verification.

Let $V$ denote the number of cast ballots and $N$ the number of candidates. Every accepted ballot encodes precisely one valid choice. The aggregate ciphertexts must produce a consistent tally. The published result must also carry cryptographic evidence that any independent observer can verify.

\section{Motivation}

Several technical advances have made the combination of these methods a realistic option.

Blockchain platforms, such as Ethereum, incorporate programmable smart contracts along with append-only event logs and deterministic execution. These elements remove one class of attack that often plagues centralized systems---namely, silent modifications to election records \cite{nakamoto2008,wood2014}.

In parallel, zk-SNARK systems have advanced to generate succinct proofs. On-chain verification remains feasible within practical smart-contract gas limits \cite{groth2016}.

This foundation is bolstered by hash functions optimized for circuits and Merkle trees. They support efficient post-election inclusion checks across large ballot sets without imposing excessive on-chain storage demands on the blockchain \cite{poseidon2021,merkle1988}.

These components together establish a viable design space. The inherent tension between preserving voter privacy and enabling public verifiability diminishes. Assurance therefore relies on cryptographic proofs and auditable execution logs instead of institutional trust.

\section{Research Objectives}

This study pursues five objectives.

\begin{itemize}
    \item to build a contract-level state machine that enforces explicit lifecycle transitions for the election;
    \item to support private ballot submission through one-hot ElGamal encryption on BabyJubJub;
    \item to use Groth16 zero-knowledge proofs to verify the validity of each vote and tally claims derived from off-chain aggregation;
    \item to develop a receipt-based audit mechanism. Voters can use their local receipt along with a published audit bundle to confirm that their ballot commitment has been correctly included. The process includes checking the consistency between the bundle root and the on-chain \texttt{merkleRoot}, without revealing any further information about the voter's identity;
    \item to evaluate the strengths of the implementation while also examining its limitations, such as remaining engineering constraints, trust assumptions, and gaps that limit higher-assurance deployment.
\end{itemize}
\section{Research Questions}

Three questions guide this study: feasibility, comparative evaluation, and gap analysis.

\textbf{RQ1:} \textit{Is it feasible to integrate encrypted one-hot ballots and zero-knowledge-proof-based checks into a browser-to-blockchain workflow without relying on trusted middleware?}

Can encryption, proof generation, and smart-contract verification operate as a single browser-launched pipeline, with no middleware positioned to tamper with ballots or interfere with verification? Chapter 4 details the implementation, and Chapter 5 evaluates it through scenario-based testing.

\textbf{RQ2:} \textit{To what extent does the proposed pipeline improve verifiability over centralized log-based auditing, particularly by enabling independent tally replay and ballot-commitment inclusion verification without server interaction?}

Two tests follow: can an independent party recompute the tally from on-chain ciphertexts and reach the same result, and can a voter confirm inclusion without contacting any server? Chapter 2 frames the comparison, and Chapter 5 provides the security analysis.

\textbf{RQ3:} \textit{Which intended security guarantees are fully achieved in the current implementation, and which remain incomplete due to practical engineering constraints?}

RQ3 separates what the prototype enforces---such as on-chain format checks rejecting malformed ballots---from what it only partially addresses, such as binding integrity between voter intent and the committed payload. Evidence appears in Chapter 5 and the traceability appendix.

\section{Scope and Boundaries}

This e-voting system is a reproducible research prototype, targeting deployment of a two-candidate election.

Circuits are compiled and tested for a fixed candidate count of $N = 2$; larger $N$ requires significantly more engineering effort and is not part of this prototype. Voter eligibility relies on an administrator-managed whitelist whose real-world correctness is outside the protocol's cryptographic scope.

\textbf{Trust assumptions.} The prototype assumes a semi-trusted administrator who follows the protocol. The admin keeps the private key safely, gives an honest whitelist, and would not release wrong results since it can be easily identified by the public. For on-chain results, people only need to trust cryptography and no institutional trust is needed.

Out of scope: coercion resistance; threshold key management; decentralized governance; formal contract verification; cross-election voter privacy.

\section{Contributions}

Four concrete outputs result from this work:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a cross-stack prototype spanning Solidity smart contracts, Circom circuits, JavaScript for frontend interaction and cryptographic calculation, and Python for backend serving and validation---compiled, deployed, and independently testable;
    \item a secure vote-submission mechanism that cryptographically links each ballot's commitment to its encrypted payload, reducing the risk of discrepancies between what is proved and what is submitted;
    \item a tally pipeline running homomorphic aggregation off-chain, verifying decryption correctness through an on-chain tally proof, and anchoring the result set with a published Merkle root;
    \item a receipt-based audit path where voters confirm ballot inclusion using a local receipt and an exported bundle, with no address lookup required during verification.
\end{itemize}

\section{Dissertation Structure}

The dissertation is divided into six chapters.

Chapter 2 reviews previous research and develops the cryptographic foundations on which the protocol is built.

Chapter 3 sets out the system model and describes the protocol's operation. It also specifies the trust assumptions that have been made.

Implementation is covered in Chapter 4, where the design decisions and the behaviour of each module are examined in detail.

Chapter 5 presents the evaluation results. These include performance measurements, requirement traceability, and a risk analysis.

Chapter 6 summarises the findings and offers a roadmap for future hardening.

Two appendices support the main text: Appendix A provides a guide to reproducing the prototype locally, and Appendix B provides the requirement traceability checklist.
