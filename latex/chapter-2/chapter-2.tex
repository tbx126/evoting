\section{Electronic Voting: Landscape and Positioning}

\subsection{Security Requirements}

These four requirements are necessary for every trustworthy e-voting system to satisfy \cite{benaloh1994,chaum2004}.

\textit{Eligibility control} ensures that only voters whose address is on the whitelist can cast a ballot. Without this, the election can be easily corrupted by a Sybil attack \cite{douceur2002}.

\textit{Ballot secrecy} means that no entity including the admin is able to reveal a single ballot. The content of each ballot is always stored with encryption. Ballot secrecy is a precondition to avoid vote buying and coercion, although many other resistance mechanisms should be adopted together \cite{juels2005}.

\textit{Individual verifiability} allows everyone to verify whether their votes are included in the final results, using a receipt and the Merkle tree given by the admin \cite{chaum2004}.

\textit{Universal verifiability} enables voters or any third-party auditor/observer to check if the released result is consistent with the set of cast ballots and requires no access to any backend system or secret key information \cite{benaloh1994}.

\subsection{Centralised E-Voting Systems}

Traditional e-voting systems use centralized architectures in which only a single entity manages the whole process. Centralized architectures have the benefit of mature software tooling, established deployment experience, straightforward operational models, relatively high efficiency. Therefore, people need to concentrate the entire basis of trust in the election operators.

In 2005, Estonia launched its internet voting platform, which is the most mature and largest deployed example to date. However, a subsequent security audit by Springall et al.\ in 2014 uncovered exploitable server-side vulnerabilities \cite{springall2014}. More critically, an absence of independently auditable evidence result in that the public have no way to verify whether the tallied results honestly reflected the cast ballots if they believe in mathematics and cryptography rather than the manager institution.

Adida proposed another design ``Helios'' in 2008 \cite{adida2008}. In this system, every encrypted ballot is published together with a decryption proof, allowing observers to verify the final count is correct and unaffected. Nevertheless, all ciphertext reach the central server before being published, so that, ballot suppression or substitution may happen without detection at that single point.

These two typical systems illustrate a recurring limitation of centralized structure: the admin of the system also governs mechanisms created to constrain it. When disputes arise, resolution depends on admin themselves instead of cryptography or auditable evidence.

\subsection{Blockchain-Based Voting Approaches}

Blockchain is a new technology that can be used in e-voting system. Blockchain platforms for example, Bitcoin \cite{nakamoto2008} and Ethereum \cite{wood2014}, provide a transparent ledger operated by deterministic execution rules. Smart contracts running on Ethereum encode constants directly in open-source code, removing opaque backend that exist in all centralized architectures. The security is provided by the miners in Ethereum community; even the owner of smart contracts is unable to change a single variable secretly.

Early research in this area usually focused on demonstrating feasibility of integrating blockchain into a part of e-voting system. McCorry et al.\ introduced the first privacy-preserving voting smart contract in 2017 \cite{mccorry2017}. In this design, asymmetric encryption is used to shield individual ballots. However, it required all voters to participate synchronously in multiple rounds, which is quite strict and unfair requirement because not everyone has so much time to vote. Hjalmarsson et al.\ subsequently demonstrated that on-chain lifecycle enforcement was viable in theory \cite{hjalmarsson2018}, but their prototype stored every ballot's contents in plaintext and incurred gas costs that increased exponentially. The larger the voter pool got, the more prohibitively expensive each step became.


\subsection{Summary and Research Gap}

In \S2.1.3, these two existing system designs show that: centralized systems lack transparency. In contrast, every step in the election is encoded into logs which are recorded on the blockchain ledger and public to everyone. However, there are two main shortcomings: some treat ballot contents as plaintext, sacrificing privacy; others employ encryption algorithms to protect votes but fail to give any evidence. The research gap motivates this dissertation.

\section{Homomorphic Encryption for Private Tally}

Paillier encryption \cite{paillier1999} is adopted in lots of e-voting systems. It performs addition directly on each component of the encoded ballot. Helios \cite{adida2008} is one of these systems. Its arithmetic nevertheless encounters a fundamental mismatch. Ciphertexts compute modulo a composite integer. zk-SNARK circuits instead require pure finite-field operations. Embedding the Paillier scheme inside a Groth16 circuit therefore calls for non-native field arithmetic. The resulting constraint count rises sharply.

ElGamal encryption offers a more compatible alternative. On elliptic-curve discrete-logarithm groups it supports additive homomorphism over encoded messages \cite{elgamal1985,hankerson2004ecc}. This work deploys the scheme on BabyJubJub \cite{eip2494}. The curve is defined over the BN254 scalar field. Native arithmetic in Groth16 circuits follows directly. No field conversion overhead materializes.

Each ballot takes the form of a one-hot vector. Component-wise aggregation of ciphertexts produces the encrypted candidate totals. Decryption applies exclusively to these aggregates. Individual ballots thus stay concealed. Aggregation scales linearly with the number of votes. It relies on nothing more than point additions. The final step of extracting integer counts from decrypted points moves off-chain to a discrete logarithm calculation.

Privacy holds provided the secret key remains uncompromised and plaintext side channels are controlled. This guarantee is weaker than threshold decryption, which tolerates partial key compromise, but requires no distributed key generation ceremony.

\section{Zero-Knowledge Proofs in Voting}

\subsection{The Problem ZKPs Address}

Zero-knowledge proofs are a kind of cryptographic techniques to give some statements without leaking any information related to them. Many non-zero-knowledge proof workflows expose sensitive information or secret data to the verifier in order to give a proof.

ZKPs separate the \emph{claim} from the \emph{evidence}. Let $x$ be a public statement and $w$ be a private witness. Define a relation $\mathcal{R}$ such that $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$ means ``$w$ is a valid witness for $x$''. A ZKP system allows a prover who knows $w$ to produce a short artifact $\pi$ that convinces any verifier that $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$, without $\pi$ leaking any information about $w$.

In this project, two instances of this pattern apply:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Vote proof}: the public statement and witness are
    \begin{align*}
    x &= (\mathit{commitment},\; H_{ct},\; \mathit{PK}_x,\; \mathit{PK}_y) \\
    w &= (\mathit{candidateId},\; \mathit{salt},\; r_0, \dots, r_{N-1},\\
       &\quad \mathit{c1X}_0,\; \mathit{c1Y}_0,\; \mathit{c2X}_0,\; \mathit{c2Y}_0,\; \dots,\; \mathit{c1X}_{N-1},\; \mathit{c1Y}_{N-1},\; \mathit{c2X}_{N-1},\; \mathit{c2Y}_{N-1})
    \end{align*}
    The relation $\mathcal{R}$ requires that: (i) $\mathit{commitment} = \mathsf{Poseidon}(\mathit{candidateId}, \mathit{salt})$, (ii) $0 \le \mathit{candidateId} < N$, (iii) each ciphertext tuple $(\mathit{c1X}_j,\mathit{c1Y}_j,\mathit{c2X}_j,\mathit{c2Y}_j)$ is a valid ElGamal encryption of one-hot bit $m_j$ where $m_j=1$ iff $j=\mathit{candidateId}$, and (iv) $H_{ct}$ matches the Poseidon hash of all ciphertext coordinates.
    \item \textbf{Tally proof}: $w = \mathit{sk}$, with public statement
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{split}
        x = (&\mathit{PK}_x,\; \mathit{PK}_y,\; \bar{C1}^{X}_0,\; \bar{C1}^{Y}_0,\; \dots,\; \bar{C1}^{X}_{N-1},\; \bar{C1}^{Y}_{N-1},\\
             &\bar{C2}^{X}_0,\; \bar{C2}^{Y}_0,\; \dots,\; \bar{C2}^{X}_{N-1},\; \bar{C2}^{Y}_{N-1},\\
             &R^{X}_0,\; R^{Y}_0,\; \dots,\; R^{X}_{N-1},\; R^{Y}_{N-1},\; \mathit{totalVotes})
        \end{split}
    \end{equation*}
    where $R_j = n_j \cdot G$ is the result point for candidate $j$, $R^{X}_j$, $R^{Y}_j$ are the coordinates of $R_j$, $n_j$ is the corresponding integer vote count, and $\mathit{totalVotes}$ is the expected sum of all candidate totals (corresponding to $V$ in Chapter 1). Here, $\mathit{PK}_x, \mathit{PK}_y$ denote affine coordinates of $\mathit{PK}$. Likewise, $\bar{C1}^{X}_j,\bar{C1}^{Y}_j$ and $\bar{C2}^{X}_j,\bar{C2}^{Y}_j$ denote affine coordinates of aggregated points $\bar{C1}_j,\bar{C2}_j$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Formal Security Properties}

A ZKP system must satisfy three properties \cite{goldwasser1989}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Completeness}: If $(x, w) \in \mathcal{R}$, then an honest prover holding $w$ always produces a proof $\pi$ that the verifier accepts. This guarantees that valid ballots and correct tallies are never falsely rejected.
    \item \textbf{Soundness}: If no valid witness $w$ exists for statement $x$, then no computationally bounded adversary can produce an accepted proof except with negligible probability. This prevents fabricated ballots or incorrect tally claims from being accepted on-chain.
    \item \textbf{Zero-Knowledge}: A simulator can produce proofs with the same distribution as real proofs without knowing $w$. This formalizes that $\pi$ reveals nothing about $w$ beyond the existence of a valid witness.
\end{enumerate}

In a zk-SNARK, soundness is computational (relying on cryptographic hardness assumptions), and non-interactivity is achieved through a structured reference string (SRS) produced in a trusted setup. The SRS provides the cryptographic parameters needed for proving and verification without any round-trip interaction.

\subsection{Arithmetic Circuits and R1CS}

Any computation over a finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$ can be expressed as an arithmetic circuit of addition and multiplication gates. A circuit is \emph{satisfiable} if there exists an assignment to all wires (public inputs, private witnesses, and intermediate values) that makes every gate equation hold. This satisfiability problem is encoded as a \emph{Rank-1 Constraint System} (R1CS).

An R1CS consists of $m$ constraints over a witness vector $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{F}_p^n$ (which includes public inputs, private witnesses, and intermediate wire values, with $s_0 = 1$ by convention):
\begin{equation}
    \langle \mathbf{a}_i,\, \mathbf{s} \rangle \;\cdot\; \langle \mathbf{b}_i,\, \mathbf{s} \rangle \;=\; \langle \mathbf{c}_i,\, \mathbf{s} \rangle, \qquad i = 1, \dots, m
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{a}_i, \mathbf{b}_i, \mathbf{c}_i \in \mathbb{F}_p^n$ are sparse selector vectors derived from the circuit topology. Each multiplication gate contributes one constraint; additions and constant multiplications are absorbed into the selector vectors at no constraint cost.

In circom, the programmer writes constraints in a domain-specific language. The compiler translates them to an \texttt{.r1cs} file (the constraint matrices $A, B, C$) and a WASM witness generator. For this project, the compiled constraint counts are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{vote\_proof}: 14{,}590 constraints, encoding commitment verification, candidate range checks, one-hot encryption validity, and ciphertext hash binding.
    \item \texttt{tally\_proof}: 16{,}786 constraints, encoding key-ownership verification, per-candidate decryption correctness, and total-vote sum consistency.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{QAP Transformation}

Groth16 operates on a polynomial encoding of the R1CS called a \emph{Quadratic Arithmetic Program} (QAP) \cite{gennaro2013}. For each constraint index $i$ and witness index $j$, define polynomials $u_j, v_j, w_j \in \mathbb{F}_p[X]$ by Lagrange interpolation through the point values $u_j(i) = a_{i,j}$, $v_j(i) = b_{i,j}$, $w_j(i) = c_{i,j}$.

Define
\begin{equation*}
A(X)=\sum_j s_j\,u_j(X), \qquad B(X)=\sum_j s_j\,v_j(X), \qquad C(X)=\sum_j s_j\,w_j(X).
\end{equation*}
At each constraint point $X=i$, interpolation gives $A(i)=\langle \mathbf{a}_i,\mathbf{s}\rangle$, $B(i)=\langle \mathbf{b}_i,\mathbf{s}\rangle$, and $C(i)=\langle \mathbf{c}_i,\mathbf{s}\rangle$. Therefore the R1CS equations imply
\begin{equation*}
A(i)\cdot B(i)-C(i)=0, \qquad i=1,\dots,m.
\end{equation*}
So the polynomial
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    P(X) \;=\; &\Bigl(\sum_j s_j\, u_j(X)\Bigr) \cdot \Bigl(\sum_j s_j\, v_j(X)\Bigr) \\
               &-\; \sum_j s_j\, w_j(X)
\end{split}
\end{equation}
satisfies $P(i)=0$ for all $i=1,\dots,m$. Since $T(X)=\prod_{i=1}^{m}(X-i)$ has exactly these roots, $T(X)$ divides $P(X)$, and equivalently there exists a quotient polynomial $H(X)$ such that:
\begin{equation}
    A(X) \cdot B(X) \;-\; C(X) \;=\; H(X) \cdot T(X)
\end{equation}
where $A, B, C$ are shorthand for the left, right, and output linear combinations of the witness polynomials. The prover's task is to commit to all polynomials evaluated at a secret point $\tau$, without knowing $\tau$ in the clear. This is achieved through the structured reference string produced in the trusted setup phase.

\subsection{The Groth16 Protocol}

Groth16 implements the QAP argument using bilinear pairings over elliptic curves. Let $\mathbb{G}_1, \mathbb{G}_2$ be groups of prime order $p$ with generators $G_1, G_2$, and let $e: \mathbb{G}_1 \times \mathbb{G}_2 \rightarrow \mathbb{G}_T$ be a non-degenerate bilinear pairing. The protocol has three phases.

\paragraph{Setup.}
A trusted party samples secrets $(\tau, \alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta) \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{F}_p^5$ and computes two structured reference strings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proving key} $\mathit{pk}_{\mathit{Groth}}$: contains $\{[\tau^i]_1\}$ for required powers, $\{[u_j(\tau)]_1\}$, $\{[v_j(\tau)]_2\}$, and blinded combinations---tens of thousands of elliptic-curve points for circuits of this scale (notation distinguished from ElGamal public key $\mathit{PK}$ and coordinates $\mathit{PK}_x,\mathit{PK}_y$).
    \item \textbf{Verification key} $\mathit{vk}$: contains $[\alpha]_1$, $[\beta]_2$, $[\gamma]_2$, $[\delta]_2$, and $\{[(\beta u_j + \alpha v_j + w_j)(\tau)/\gamma]_1\}$ for the public signal indices.
\end{itemize}

The secrets $(\tau, \alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta)$ are then irrecoverably destroyed---these are the ``toxic waste'' of the trusted setup. If any value were leaked, an adversary could forge proofs for false statements without possessing a valid witness. This risk motivates the \emph{Powers of Tau} multi-party computation ceremony \cite{powersoftau2017}, in which many independent participants each contribute fresh randomness and prove they discarded their contribution. The final artifact is secure as long as at least one participant acted honestly.

\paragraph{Prove.}
Given the full witness $\mathbf{s}$ and the proving key, the prover samples random blinding scalars $\rho, \sigma \overset{\$}{\leftarrow} \mathbb{F}_p$ and computes a three-element proof $\pi = (\pi_A, \pi_B, \pi_C)$ (distinct from ElGamal encryption randomness $r_j$):
\begin{align}
    \pi_A &= \bigl[\alpha + A(\tau) + \rho\delta\bigr]_1 \\
    \pi_B &= \bigl[\beta + B(\tau) + \sigma\delta\bigr]_2 \\
    \pi_C &= \left[\frac{C_{\mathit{priv}}(\tau) + H(\tau)T(\tau)}{\delta}\right]_1 + \sigma \cdot \pi_A \\
          &\quad + \rho \cdot \bigl[\beta + B(\tau) + \sigma\delta\bigr]_1 - \rho\sigma \cdot [\delta]_1 \notag
\end{align}
where $[\cdot]_k$ denotes the elliptic-curve point $(\cdot) \cdot G_k$, and $C_{\mathit{priv}}$ denotes the private-witness portion of the $C$ polynomial. The blinding factors $\rho$ and $\sigma$ enforce Groth16's perfect zero-knowledge property: a simulator can generate proofs with the same distribution as real proofs without knowing the witness.

\paragraph{Verify.}
The verifier checks one bilinear pairing equation combining proof elements with verification-key elements:
\begin{equation}
    e(\pi_A,\; \pi_B) \;=\; e\bigl([\alpha]_1,\; [\beta]_2\bigr) \;\cdot\; e\!\left(\sum_{i=0}^{l} a_i \cdot \mathit{vk}_i,\; [\gamma]_2\right) \;\cdot\; e(\pi_C,\; [\delta]_2)
\end{equation}
where $l$ is the number of explicit public inputs (excluding the constant term), $\mathit{vk}_i = [(\beta u_i + \alpha v_i + w_i)(\tau)/\gamma]_1$ are precomputed verification-key elements, $a_0 = 1$ is the constant term, and $a_1,\dots,a_l$ are the public-input values. This single equation encodes the correctness of $A(\tau) \cdot B(\tau) = C(\tau) + H(\tau) \cdot T(\tau)$ through the algebraic properties of the pairing, without recomputing the polynomials at $\tau$. A proof is accepted if and only if this equation holds.

\subsection{On-Chain Verification Cost}

The BN254 curve (also called BN128) is natively supported by the EVM as a precompiled contract at address \texttt{0x08} for pairing computations. Under EIP-1108 \cite{eip1108}, the gas cost model is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathit{gas}_{\mathit{pairing}} = 45{,}000 + 34{,}000 \times k
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the number of pairing pairs. The Groth16 verification equation requires $k = 4$ pairs (corresponding to the four terms in the equation above), yielding $45{,}000 + 34{,}000 \times 4 = 181{,}000$ gas for the pairing check alone. Crucially, this pairing-check cost is \emph{independent of the number of circuit constraints}: a 14{,}590-constraint vote circuit and a 100{,}000-constraint circuit incur identical pairing gas. The public-input linear combination slightly increases the cost which is proportional to the number of public inputs. This feature makes Groth16 really suitable for complicated ZKP calculations that should be computed on-chain.

\subsection{Implementation Pipeline}

Translating an abstract proof relation into a deployable on-chain verifier involves four stages, each of which introduces specific artefacts and trust considerations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Circuit specification.} The prover's relation $\mathcal{R}$ is expressed as an arithmetic circuit written in the Circom domain-specific language \cite{circomdocs}. Library templates from circomlib supply reusable components for BabyJubJub point arithmetic, Poseidon hashing, range checks, and conditional logic. The Circom compiler transforms the high-level description into two outputs: an \texttt{.r1cs} file encoding the constraint matrices $(A, B, C)$ and a WebAssembly (WASM) witness generator that, given concrete inputs, computes the full witness vector $\mathbf{s}$.

    \item \textbf{Trusted setup.} The Groth16 proving system requires a circuit-specific structured reference string (SRS). Construction proceeds in two phases. Phase~1 uses a universal Powers of Tau ceremony artefact (\texttt{pot16\_final.ptau}), which supports circuits of up to $2^{16}$ constraints and whose security rests on the assumption that at least one ceremony participant destroyed their toxic waste. Phase~2 specialises the universal parameters to the particular R1CS via snarkjs, producing a circuit-specific proving key (\texttt{.zkey} file, approximately 7--10\,MB per circuit in this implementation). The corresponding verification key is extracted from the same \texttt{.zkey} and embedded into the Solidity verifier contract generated in Stage~4.

    \item \textbf{Proof generation.} At proving time the WASM witness generator constructs $\mathbf{s}$ from the public inputs and private witnesses; snarkjs then evaluates the QAP polynomials at the secret evaluation point encoded in the proving key and computes the three-element proof $(\pi_A, \pi_B, \pi_C)$. In local Node.js benchmarks for the instantiated $N{=}2$ circuits, vote-proof generation takes approximately 0.6--1.1\,s; browser-side WASM execution exhibits comparable latency, with moderate variation depending on device hardware and caching behaviour. Chapter~5 reports detailed measurements.

    \item \textbf{Verifier export.} Finally, snarkjs generates a self-contained Solidity contract that hard-codes the verification key constants and implements the pairing-check equation (Equation~2.6) as direct calls to the EVM BN254 precompiles, thereby eliminating any runtime parsing or key-loading overhead.
\end{enumerate}

One important trust property of this pipeline is that the code for the circuits is public: anyone may read \texttt{vote\_proof.circom} and \texttt{tally\_proof.circom}, recompile them and ensure that the output R1CS is consistent with their respective \texttt{.zkey} artifacts if those artifacts are published alongside the deployment. This auditability---along with the verifiable Powers of Tau ceremony transcript---means that trust is put on the cryptographic hardness assumption as well as the setup ceremony, not on the circuit author's integrity.

\subsection{ZKP Role in This Dissertation}

There are three different proof instances used and they form the following two circuits file:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{ZKP1 (commitment correctness)}: proves $\mathit{commitment} = \mathsf{Poseidon}(\mathit{candidateId}, \mathit{salt})$ without revealing $\mathit{candidateId}$ or $\mathit{salt}$.
    \item \textbf{ZKP2 (one-hot validity)}: proves that the submitted ElGamal ciphertexts encrypt precisely the one-hot vector $\mathbf{m}$ consistent with the committed candidate, without revealing $\mathbf{m}$ or the per-component randomness. Because ZKP1 and ZKP2 share witness variables (both depend on $\mathit{candidateId}$), they are compiled into a single \texttt{vote\_proof} circuit. A single proof simultaneously establishes both properties.
    \item \textbf{ZKP3 (tally decryption correctness)}: proves that for each candidate $j$, the published result point $R_j = n_j \cdot G$ equals the point obtained by decrypting the aggregated ciphertext under $\mathit{sk}$, and that $\mathit{sk}$ is consistent with the public key $\mathit{PK} = \mathit{sk} \cdot G$. The integer vote counts are derived off-chain from these curve points via discrete-log recovery, and are submitted to the contract separately; the circuit proves the correctness of the curve-point representations, not the integer values directly. This circuit is necessarily separate because it operates over the aggregated ciphertext state, which is only defined after all votes have been collected.
\end{enumerate}

The on-chain verifier contracts (\texttt{VoteVerifier.sol}, \texttt{TallyVerifier.sol}), generated automatically by snarkjs, each implement the pairing-check equation as a pure Solidity function. The main \texttt{Voting} contract call them as a must-have precondition of \texttt{castVote} and \texttt{updateTallyResults} respectively, enforcing the encoded circuit relations before state updates. Chapter~5 discusses some of the binding gaps that remain to be hardened for production assurance.

\section{Commitments, Hashing, and Merkle Auditability}

In this design, the commitment is treated as the binding receipt of encrypted ballot. The voter calculates a hash over candidate ID and a random salt value. Because of the preimage resistance of hash functions, there is no way for others to find the plaintext via the hash value.

Instead of SHA-256 or other popular traditional hash functions which has been widely used in bitcoin or other blockchain systems, Poseidon \cite{poseidon2021} is adopted in this scenario because its optimization for arithmetic circuits makes it more efficient and suitable.

In the commitment Merkle tree \cite{merkle1988}, every two commitments recursively generated a new commitment until there is only one commitment which then is the merkleRoot. Through a Merkle tree, the auditability is expanded to the full ballot set. The complexity of inclusion proofs are logarithmic with the number of votes, hence verification is affordable and acceptable even for a large amount of ballots.

The protocol supports two mutually independent verification mechanisms. Each voter is able to confirm, with high assurance, that their own commitment is correctly included in the Merkle tree below the published root, while obtaining no information whatsoever about any other voter's identity or commitment. Individually, an outside auditor is able to recalculate the root based on published entries in some deterministic order and then compares that with the announced root as well as the onchain root value.

Chapter~3 turns these foundations into a practical protocol model, on which Chapter~4 is built.
